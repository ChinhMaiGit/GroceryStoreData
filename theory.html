Theory

The intuition behind the construction — condensed from the full paper

This page is a web-friendly walkthrough of the ideas behind grocery-sim, condensed but not watered down — every equation below is copied from the same model that generates the data, not a simplified stand-in for it. For the complete derivations, every calibrated parameter, and the causal-graph figures, see the full paper (PDF).

The problem with the usual two options

Hand an analyst a real business’s spreadsheet, and she can check whether her conclusions sound reasonable — but not whether they are true, because nobody, not her, not the business itself, actually knows the mechanism that produced the numbers. Hand her a spreadsheet generated by drawing every column from a convenient distribution, and she can check her work against the generating code, but there’s nothing underneath worth discovering: no owner who misread a signal, no competitor who mattered less than everyone assumed, no honest mess in the paperwork to clean up before the real question can even be asked.

grocery-sim is a third option: build the business first, as a world with people, budgets, and a street, and only afterward let the paperwork fall out of what that world actually did. The result is a dataset messy enough to feel real, where every number still has a traceable cause — a conclusion can be checked not just for plausibility but for truth.

Two layers, and the rule that never gets broken

The world is built from two layers that are never allowed to blend into one another:

  • The micro layer — decisions. A customer choosing between two cartons of milk. An owner choosing where to open a store before a single customer has walked through the door. The same owner choosing, later, whether to hire, and whether to expand, under capital he does not have room to lose. Every one of these is a real decision, built from plain human intuition first and only then written as a precise mathematical object.
  • The macro layer — the script. The weather, the calendar, and the macroeconomic shocks are scripted in advance and never listen to anything the simulated people do. A supply shock happens on a fixed date whether or not the owner reacts well to it.

The distinction is not a modeling convenience — it’s the whole point. It’s what lets a later reader ask what would have happened without the storm, or without the competitor, and get an answer that is exactly true rather than merely estimated.

The customer’s purchase decision

Every product \(s\) offers customer \(i\) a utility

\[ U_{is} = \theta_{ic} + a_s + \gamma\left(1 - \left|b_i - \text{BrandLevel}_s\right|\right) - \beta_i\,\text{Price}_s , \]

where \(\theta_{ic}\) is her current need for the category, \(b_i\) is her brand affinity, \(\text{BrandLevel}_s\) is the product’s position on that same spectrum, \(\gamma\) sets how much a good brand match is worth, \(\beta_i\) is her own price sensitivity, and \(a_s \sim \text{Normal}(0, 0.8)\) is an unmodeled per-product appeal — packaging, familiarity, the thousand reasons one product outsells its shelf neighbor for no principled reason at all. Alongside every stocked product sits an outside option (buy nothing here, or buy elsewhere), and the actual choice is drawn as

\[ \text{choice} \sim \text{Categorical}\big(\text{softmax}(U_{is_1}, \ldots, U_{is_K}, U_0)\big) . \]

The softmax form isn’t a convenient approximation. If each product’s utility is disturbed by an independent Gumbel shock and the customer simply picks the option with the highest noisy utility, the resulting choice probabilities are exactly the softmax of the mean utilities — McFadden’s random utility result. It’s also exactly the model a real discrete-choice analyst would fit to purchase histories, so grading a later exercise against the true parameters is a fair comparison of like against like.

Need itself, \(\theta_{ict}\), is not a free-floating random draw — it comes from a pantry that drains and refills:

\[ P_{ic,t+1} \overset{\leftarrow}{=} \max(0,\ P_{ict} - r_{ict}) + \text{units purchased}, \qquad \theta_{ict} = \alpha\,\frac{\max(0,\ P^{\text{tgt}}_{ic} - P_{ict})}{P^{\text{tgt}}_{ic}} , \]

the shortfall against a target of ten days’ comfortable cover. The drain rate \(r_{ict}\) scales with the season’s demand modifier rather than adding to desire directly, so a hot week doesn’t make a household want ice cream in the abstract — it makes the household eat the ice cream in the freezer faster. When the chosen product is out of stock, the original intent is logged as a latent truth under cause stockout before the softmax is re-evaluated with that product removed — an honest ledger of demand that never appears in the sales data at all.

The owner’s opening decision

The true demand potential of a location for a category is \(D_{cl} = \text{Households}_l \cdot \mu_c\) — ground truth nobody in the model observes directly. The owner’s belief is that truth seen through a noisy, mildly optimistic lens,

\[ \hat{D}_{cl} = D_{cl}\,e^{\delta_c}, \qquad \delta_c \sim \text{Normal}(0.10,\ 0.25) , \]

on the log scale because people misjudge quantities in ratios, not absolute units, and the positive mean encodes a documented behavioral regularity: new owners tend to be mildly overoptimistic. That single choice is what causes the store to systematically overstock in its early months — the size of the overstocking is computable exactly from the belief error, not asserted as an unexplained fact about the data.

With beliefs, costs, and a handful of standing policies fixed — a markup \(m_c\), a safety-stock fraction \(\eta = 0.30\) of believed monthly demand, a cap \(\rho = 0.05\) on any one product’s share of its category — the opening decision is a mixed integer linear program:

\[ \max \;\; \sum_{c}\sum_{s \in S_c} (1+m_c)\,\text{Cost}_s \cdot u_s \;-\; \text{TotalCost}_0 \]

subject to, among others,

\[ \sum_l y_l = 1, \qquad u_s \leq q_s, \qquad \sum_{s \in S_c} u_s \leq \sum_l y_l\,\hat{D}_{cl}, \qquad \text{TotalCost}_0 \leq \text{InitialCapital}_0 , \]

with \(y_l \in \{0,1\}\) the chosen location, \(q_s \geq 0\) the quantity purchased, and \(u_s \geq 0\) the quantity the owner believes he’ll sell. A mixed integer program is the right tool because the decision is genuinely joint: location and listing choices are binary, purchase quantities are continuous, and every constraint linking them is linear once the location is fixed. Because the belief error carries a positive mean, the optimum systematically overstocks relative to the truth — so when an analyst later finds bloated inventory in the cost sheets, there’s a real mechanism behind it, not an injected anomaly.

Day-to-day adaptive policy

Once the shop is open, restocking is sized off a trailing four-week average of the owner’s own recorded sales,

\[ \hat{D}^{\text{op}}_{cw} \overset{\leftarrow}{=} \frac{1}{4}\sum_{j=1}^{4} \text{Sales}_{c,w-j} , \qquad \text{Target}_{sw} = \left(\tfrac{R+L}{7} + \eta\,\tfrac{30}{7}\right) \hat{D}^{\text{op}}_{cw}\,\tilde{\sigma}_{sw} , \]

with \(R = 7\) days between reviews and \(L = 2\) days of lead time. The rule carries two realistic flaws by construction: it’s backward-looking, so a rising trend is always underestimated by the average of the weeks before it rose, and it’s blind to censoring, since the sales it averages are themselves truncated by the store’s own past stockouts — a product that stocks out repeatedly reports weak sales, earns a smaller share of the next order, and stocks out again.

Perishable stock spoils overnight,

\[ \text{Spoiled}_{st} \sim \text{Binomial}\big(\text{OnHand}_{st},\ \min(0.35,\ \lambda^d_{c(s)}\,f_{c(s),t})\big) , \]

with the spoilage factor \(f_{ct}\) built log-additively from a heat term (strongest for ambient perishables), a cold-chain strain term tied to the macro shocks below, and an idiosyncratic batch-luck term.

The expansion decision

From the start of year two, the owner retains a share \(\rho_{RE} = 0.5\) of every profitable month’s after-tax result, drawing the rest as income and neither drawing nor retaining anything in a loss month:

\[ \text{draw}_m = (1-\rho_{RE})\,\max(0, \pi_m) . \]

Once retained earnings and available cash both cross fixed thresholds,

\[ \text{RE}_m \geq K_{\text{exp}}, \qquad \text{cash}_m - \text{tax\_reserve}_m \geq C_{\text{exp}} , \]

the owner expands on the first day of the following month. This is a threshold decision framed around what he can afford to lose, not a discounted-cash-flow forecast — closer to what entrepreneurship research calls effectuation than to a textbook net-present-value calculation. A counterfactual twin that disables the threshold entirely shows the expansion’s true cost across its first fourteen months substantially exceeds the margin the extra capacity generates: the fixed cost of an extra wage is paid in full while the extra revenue is diluted by the ordinary grocery margin.

The macro layer: weather, demand, and cost shocks

Temperature follows a deterministic annual cycle plus a persistent stochastic anomaly,

\[ T_t = \mu_T(t) + \varepsilon_t, \qquad \mu_T(t) = 12 + 9\cos\!\left(\tfrac{2\pi(t-200)}{365}\right), \qquad \varepsilon_t = \phi\,\varepsilon_{t-1} + \text{Normal}(0,\sigma_\varepsilon) , \]

with \(\phi = 0.7\) — an autoregressive form chosen because it’s the maximum entropy stationary process consistent with the two facts about weather worth encoding: a stable typical spread, and warm or cold spells that persist for days rather than resetting each morning.

A daily, per-category demand modifier is built log-additively from three genuinely distinct causal channels,

\[ \log M_{ct} = a_c \cos\!\left(\tfrac{2\pi(t-200)}{365}\right) + \kappa_c\, z_t + h_c\, H_t , \]

where \(z_t\) is the standardized temperature anomaly and \(H_t\) flags days before a major holiday: \(a_c\) is the predictable seasonal amplitude, \(\kappa_c\) the pure weather effect, \(h_c\) the holiday stock-up surge. One category is deliberately given all-zero loadings, so nothing in preferences makes it more or less wanted at any time of year — yet its observed sales still aren’t flat, because every category draws from the same household budget, and other categories’ seasonal surges crowd it out. An analyst who stops at an apparent seasonal pattern here has mistaken a budget-mediation effect for a genuine preference effect.

Wholesale cost shocks arrive as a marked point process, \(K \sim \text{Poisson}(1.5)\) events at \(\tau_k \sim \text{Uniform}(1,365)\), each with peak magnitude \(\zeta_k \sim \text{LogNormal}(\log\zeta_0,\ 0.3)\) and a ramp-up/decay trajectory

\[ g_k(t) = \zeta_k \cdot \min\!\left(1,\ \tfrac{t-\tau_k}{14}\right) \cdot \exp\!\left(-\tfrac{(t-\tau_k-14)_+}{\rho_k}\right) , \]

ramping over two weeks and decaying exponentially after — neither a permanent level change nor a transient blip, but a slow wave a forecaster genuinely has to track. Every affected category’s cost multiplier then assembles the inflation drift and every live shock,

\[ \log \text{CostMult}_{ct} = \frac{\pi t}{365} + \sum_k g_k(t)\,\mathbb{1}[c \in A_k] . \]

The owner never observes this directly — he sees only supplier invoices, tracked as an exponentially smoothed trend, and moves the shelf tag only once that trend has drifted more than three percent from the current price (menu-cost pricing). The scripted energy crisis also squeezes household budgets through a separate mechanism, making it a subtly invalid instrument whose bias is, in principle, computable exactly — a trap left in on purpose.

Taxation

Value-added tax applies at a category-specific rate \(r_c(t)\); a flat \(20\%\) profit tax applies only to a positive annual surplus,

\[ \text{ProfitTax} = r^{\pi}\cdot\max(0,\ \text{ProfitBeforeTax}), \qquad r^\pi = 0.20 . \]

One piece of this is provably harmless rather than merely assumed away: because profit tax is a flat rate on a positive base,

\[ \arg\max_{\text{decisions}} (1-r^\pi)\,\pi(\text{decisions}) = \arg\max_{\text{decisions}} \pi(\text{decisions}) , \]

so an owner who ignores profit tax when making ordinary operating decisions loses nothing relative to one who optimizes around it. No such guarantee holds for value-added tax, whose category-specific rate changes relative prices across categories — a gap the owner never exploits, deliberately left open for a later prescriptive analysis to find.

Why the split makes counterfactuals free

Because the macro layer never listens to the micro layer, a counterfactual world costs almost nothing to produce: edit the script — delete the scripted shock, disable the expansion threshold, remove the competitor entirely — and replay the identical daily market mechanism with every other random draw held fixed. The difference between the original run and the edited run is the causal effect of the edit, not merely an estimate of it.

This only works because every random draw is keyed by the stable identity of the entity it concerns — a specific customer on a specific day, a specific product on a specific night — using independently spawned child streams, rather than one long sequential stream. If draws were sequential, deleting a single event partway through the year would shift every subsequent draw, contaminating the comparison with reshuffled randomness that has nothing to do with the edit. Keying every draw by identity means a customer untouched by the edit reproduces the identical decision, bit for bit, in both runs — the common random numbers (CRN) technique from the simulation literature, load-bearing rather than retrofitted.

Two concrete twins in the three-year story cash this out into real numbers: one twin disables the expansion decision entirely; comparing it to the real run shows the expansion’s true cost over its first fourteen months substantially exceeds the margin it generates. A second twin removes the competitor entirely; comparing it to the real run prices exactly what the competitor cost the owner, as opposed to what he believes it cost him. The capital decision, it turns out, very often costs the business more than the competitive entry — even though the competitor is the more visible, more emotionally salient event to an owner living through it. That’s exactly why an instructor can hand a learner the owner’s own conviction that the competitor is to blame, and let the twins settle the question instead.

The three profit figures

With a full year performed, three profit figures become computable side by side:

  • \(\pi_{\text{believed}}\) — the opening MILP’s own objective value.
  • \(\pi_{\text{realized}}\) — what the ledger actually shows, after a full year of realistic behavior.
  • \(\pi_{\text{oracle}}\) — what a perfectly informed operator could have earned: same world, same script, true demand in place of a censored, backward-looking forecast.

The two gaps between them are exact differences between fully computed runs of the same mechanism, not estimates:

\[ \Delta_{\text{optimism}} = \pi_{\text{realized}} - \pi_{\text{believed}} \qquad \Delta_{\text{analytics}} = \pi_{\text{oracle}} - \pi_{\text{realized}} \]

The first measures the cost of the owner’s initial overoptimism; the second measures the value of better information and better decisions — the single number this entire construction exists to make credible by actually computing it, rather than asserting a plausible size for it.

The recording layer’s own algebra

The paperwork sits in a third layer, neither decision nor script: low-rate, independently keyed defects injected only at export, never touching the simulation’s internal state. Let \(D_{st}\) be a product’s cumulative book-minus-true discrepancy on day \(t\) — pushed up by duplicate invoices and unrecorded spoilage, down by missing invoices and duplicate receipts. The inventory figure an analyst actually sees is book stock, not true stock,

\[ B_{st} = S_{st} + D_{st} - \sum_{c\le t} A_{sc} , \]

where \(A_{sc}\) is the cumulative correction posted at each monthly physical count, snapping book stock back to physical truth before it begins drifting anew. Every defect is written simultaneously to a separate answer key, so reconciling the messy records back to the truth is itself gradable against a known answer — the underlying simulated reality this section’s own machinery depends on is never disturbed by paperwork noise that exists only in the documents an analyst receives.
